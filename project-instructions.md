# Project Instructions

## 1. Project name or working title
**The Serpent of Österlen**

## 2. Type of fiction project
A fast-paced **conspiracy thriller / historical thriller / mystery thriller** with strong symbolic, archaeological, and religious layers.

## 3. Core idea in one sentence
Write an original, high-velocity thriller set on **Österlen in Skåne** in which a present-day chain of violence, symbols, and concealed history leads into a dangerous secret connected to **Old Norse myth, ritual memory, and people who once believed in it - and may still do so in some form today**.

## 4. Primary target audience
Adult readers who enjoy intelligent page-turners with riddles, historical depth, symbolic clues, real places, compressed time, and a constant sense that each discovery opens onto a larger hidden system.

## 5. Most important final deliverable
The essential final deliverable is a **complete thriller novel manuscript**: full-length, coherent from beginning to end, dramatically sound, genuinely suspenseful, and structurally capable of standing as a readable first complete version rather than a fragment, outline, or partial draft. The target length is **approximately 250 pages**, which for planning purposes should be treated as **about 75,000 words**.

## 6. Genre and tonal direction
This project should be executed as a **high-tempo intellectual thriller** with a strong mystery engine, escalating danger, symbolic and historical decoding, and a clear sense of conspiratorial pressure. The tonal blend should be:

- urgent and propulsive
- dark but not nihilistic
- atmospheric and place-anchored
- intelligent but accessible
- realistic in the treatment of geography, history, archaeology, belief traditions, institutions, and practical movement through space
- loaded with mystery, but not vague

The novel should feel local in setting and texture, yet large in implication. It should avoid generic Scandinavian slowness. The pace should remain tight, with short or tightly driven chapters, recurring cliffhangers, layered reveals, and a strong ticking-clock effect.

The story should inherit the **functional thriller qualities** described in `thriller-style.md`: a shocking early trigger, a knowledgeable but vulnerable protagonist, a clue chain built from symbols, places, texts, or artifacts, a large idea-conflict beneath the surface plot, compressed time, and a finale that resolves both external danger and the protagonist's deeper dilemma. fileciteturn2file0

## 7. Central ingredients the story should contain
The novel should contain, at minimum, the following core ingredients:

- a central mystery of real consequence
- a chain of clues tied to **symbols, inscriptions, local history, landscape features, old documents, ritual traces, place-names, mythic references, and/or archaeological remains**
- one or more antagonistic forces operating through secrecy, pressure, manipulation, violence, or institutional capture
- strong use of **real Österlen locations** as more than scenery; they must actively shape the plot
- Old Norse thematic material treated seriously and accurately enough to feel grounded rather than fantasy-like
- a present-day conflict involving what survives, mutates, or is strategically reused from older belief systems
- a protagonist whose expertise matters to the mystery
- at least one important counterpart / partner / influence character who challenges the protagonist's habits of thought
- staggered revelations that repeatedly force reinterpretation of the mystery
- a time-critical progression toward a dangerous public, ritual, institutional, or symbolic event
- a final revelation and final decision that are both action-driven and idea-driven

Where appropriate, draw on real Österlen sites such as **Ale's Stones / Ales stenar near Kåseberga, Glimmingehus, Kivik's King's Grave / Bredarör, and the Stenshuvud area**, not as tourist decoration but as narratively functional locations. Official and heritage-oriented sources describe Ales stenar as a 67-metre stone ship above Kåseberga dated broadly to the early Iron Age, Glimmingehus as the best-preserved medieval manor in Scandinavia begun in 1499, and Bredarör in Kivik as one of Sweden's most remarkable Bronze Age monuments. citeturn434556search1turn102130search7turn102130search15

## 8. Reader experience the story should produce
The novel should make the reader feel:

- constant forward pull
- curiosity at the end of almost every chapter
- pleasure in decoding patterns
- fascination with place, myth, and hidden continuity
- rising stakes from local mystery to much larger consequence
- the sensation that intelligence matters, but so do courage, trust, and moral judgment
- that the book is both a thriller and an encounter with a deeply textured landscape

The ideal effect is: **"I need to keep reading because every answer changes the shape of the question."**

## 9. Known requirements and constraints
These constraints are fixed and should not be casually overridden:

- The novel must be **original** and must not imitate any specific author's exact prose, sentence habits, scene design, or character templates.
- It must, however, operate **in the same broad functional mode** as described in `thriller-style.md`.
- The setting must be **Österlen in Skåne**, not a vague fictionalized Scandinavian coast.
- Place descriptions must be **careful, concrete, geographically believable, and knowledge-based**.
- Historical, archaeological, and religious references must be handled with **serious factual discipline**.
- The Old Norse dimension must remain within a **realistic thriller framework**. Do not turn the novel into fantasy.
- The novel must have **fast pacing**.
- The story should be built with close attention to both `thriller-style.md` and `style-guide.pdf`, both of which are located in the project root and must be treated as authoritative guidance.
- If uncertainty arises about place facts, archaeology, historical chronology, ritual terminology, travel logic, weather plausibility, or institutional detail, research must be performed before drafting or finalizing the relevant material.
- Do not invent specific present-day religious groups, practices, legal statuses, or archaeological claims when verifiable sources are available.
- Any contemporary continuation of Old Norse belief must be handled carefully: it may be present, but it must not be sensationalized through false claims. Note that there are present-day groups in Sweden such as **Samfundet Forn Sed Sverige**, which describes itself as a registered religious organisation and a living spiritual path rooted in Nordic heathen and folk custom rather than a theatrical recreation of the past. citeturn379668search2turn379668search8

## 10. Worldbuilding, setting, and knowledge world
The knowledge world for this project includes:

- Österlen geography and local movement between villages, roads, coast, cliffs, beaches, fields, harbours, churches, ruins, museums, manor settings, and heritage sites
- Scandinavian archaeology, especially southern Sweden / Skåne
- Bronze Age, Iron Age, Viking Age, medieval afterlives, and later reinterpretations of the past
- Old Norse mythology, ritual language, cosmology, symbols, mythic figures, and the difference between medieval textual sources, reconstructed scholarship, folklore afterlives, and present-day spiritual reinterpretations
- institutional environments such as archives, churches, museums, local history collections, universities, heritage authorities, landowners, or cultural foundations
- the psychology of belief, memory, secrecy, and reinterpretation

The manuscript should make rigorous use of the principle from `style-guide.pdf` that a story world becomes convincing when the people, professions, social details, and background characters genuinely fit that setting rather than functioning as generic filler. It also stresses that background characters should reinforce the story goal and that setting-specific research matters. fileciteturn2file10

### Research expectations for the knowledge world
Before finalizing outline, scene plans, or chapters, perform targeted research on:

1. **Österlen locations**: topography, settlement patterns, coastal features, distances, travel times, weather exposure, seasonal rhythms, and what can actually be seen, reached, or plausibly done at each location.
2. **Key sites used in the plot**: opening hours if relevant, public access, terrain, sightlines, nearby roads, parking, footpaths, approach routes, historical dating, and scholarly uncertainty where applicable.
3. **Old Norse material**: prioritize academically responsible sources and distinguish between primary textual traditions, archaeological evidence, later folklore, popular misconceptions, and current religious reinterpretations.
4. **Modern continuity of belief**: if the story includes people who presently practice or identify with modern forms of Old Norse belief, represent them with nuance and factual restraint.

When possible, prefer official and high-trust sources such as:

- national or regional heritage authorities
- museums and site operators
- national park / conservation bodies
- official tourism and local history institutions
- reputable scholarly material
- contemporary religious communities' own public descriptions when depicting their present-day self-understanding

## 11. Character framework
The exact cast may be invented freely, but the following framework should guide creation.

### Protagonist
The protagonist should be a highly readable lead for this kind of novel:

- intelligent
- pattern-sensitive
- professionally or intellectually credible
- emotionally limited in some important way
- vulnerable under pressure
- more at home with symbols, history, texts, archives, archaeology, religion, folklore, landscape-reading, or interpretive analysis than with violence
- forced into danger anyway

The protagonist should have expertise that directly unlocks the plot. That expertise must not be ornamental.

### Essential counterpart / partner / influence figure
There should be at least one major counterpart who:

- carries a different method of reading events
- challenges the protagonist's assumptions
- matters emotionally and strategically
- contributes knowledge or access the protagonist lacks
- has reasons not to trust easy explanations

This aligns with `style-guide.pdf`, which emphasizes that the emotional thread of the novel depends not only on the main character but also on an essential counterpart / influence character who pressures the main character's worldview and makes change or resistance meaningful. fileciteturn1file1

### Antagonist and larger opposing structure
The antagonist should be more than a killer or brute. The opposition should ideally combine:

- a person or small core group
- a larger institutional, ideological, financial, hereditary, religious, or reputational structure that helps conceal the truth

The antagonist's worldview should be intelligible. Their motives should connect to preservation, revelation, control, inheritance, cultural power, or the management of dangerous knowledge.

## 12. Style requirements and inspiration at the functional level
The intended reading experience should be shaped by the following **functional** traits:

- short or tightly driven chapters
- frequent cliffhangers with real narrative value
- immediate escalation after the opening shock
- clue-based progression
- repeated reinterpretation of prior facts
- dramatic use of real places
- interweaving of learning and danger
- cinematic forward motion
- puzzle pressure without turning the novel into a mere game
- controlled exposition: informative, but always in service of suspense
- an intelligent thriller feel rather than pulp chaos

`thriller-style.md` specifically stresses a protagonist who is both expert and exposed, an opening violent or shocking incident that immediately raises three large questions, clues that must simultaneously advance plot, convey knowledge, and deepen theme, a large idea-conflict under the surface plot, compressed time, and short-chapter suspense. These principles should be followed closely. fileciteturn2file6turn2file8

At the same time, `style-guide.pdf` should govern structural soundness: clear story goal, consequence, requirements, forewarnings, costs, dividends, prerequisites, preconditions, meaningful climax, and coherent dramatic functions across the cast. Characters should be distinct, believable, and three-dimensional, with specific motives, methods, and evaluative habits. fileciteturn1file1

## 14. Definition of done
The project should only be considered finished when all of the following are true:

1. A **complete novel manuscript** exists, not just notes, synopsis, or sample chapters.
2. The manuscript reads as a unified story with a clear beginning, escalation, midpoint redefinition, late-stage tightening, climactic decision, and resonant resolution.
3. The mystery functions at both levels:
   - immediate plot mystery
   - deeper thematic / historical / ideological mystery
4. Österlen is rendered with enough specificity that the setting feels lived in, navigable, and research-based rather than generic.
5. Old Norse material is handled with clear discipline and without lazy myth mashups.
6. The contemporary dimension of belief, if included, is portrayed with restraint and factual seriousness.
7. Chapters consistently generate forward drive.
8. The protagonist, counterpart, and antagonist all feel dramatically necessary.
9. The manuscript has been revised for logic, continuity, geography, chronology, clue placement, exposition control, and tonal consistency.
10. The result feels like an **original thriller novel with related functional DNA**, not an imitation of any single existing author.
